What I built, in plain English.

Here is the version without the resume buzzwords. For each project, I care about three things: what problem I was trying to solve, what the code is actually doing, and what I learned from making it work.

featured build

FaceFrame

Buying glasses online still feels weirdly primitive, so I wanted to make it feel closer to trying frames on in real life.

Why I built it I wanted to remove the "I have no idea if these will look good on me" problem.
What the code is doing It uses a MediaPipe face mesh, OpenCV overlays, and a face-shape classifier to place frames in realtime and make smarter recommendations.
What I learned People do not care how fancy the model is if the overlay feels laggy, misaligned, or untrustworthy.
MediaPipe OpenCV TensorFlow startup
full-stack ai tool

ResumeIQ

A lot of resume advice is vague, so I built something that tells people what to fix instead of just telling them to "improve their resume."

Why I built it I wanted a tool that gives concrete feedback fast, especially for students who do not have a mentor reviewing every draft.
What the code is doing It takes a PDF, extracts the text, sends a structured prompt to Claude, and turns the result into scores, strengths, missing keywords, and specific edits.
What I learned AI feels way more trustworthy when you force it into a clean structure instead of letting it ramble.
Flask Claude API pdf parsing frontend
computer vision

Facial Emotion Recognition

I wanted to see what it actually takes to move from a model that works in a notebook to one that behaves live on camera.

Why I built it I was curious about where computer vision stops being theory and starts dealing with messy reality.
What the code is doing A CNN trained on FER-2013 runs on webcam frames after MediaPipe finds the face, then OpenCV draws the result live.
What I learned Realtime systems expose every shortcut you took while training, especially once lighting and movement enter the picture.
CNN Keras OpenCV webcam

Read this if you feel lost.

I failed Calculus BC twice in high school. I passed it the third time.

That did not happen because I suddenly became a genius on attempt three. It happened because I got more honest. I stopped treating struggle like proof that I did not belong. I asked for help. I studied with less ego. I kept showing up after it stopped being fun.

I still know what it feels like to sit with a bad grade, feel behind other people, and wonder if maybe everyone else is built for this in a way that I am not. That feeling is real. It is also not the truth.

If you are in the middle of a rough semester, a failure streak, or a chapter where nothing seems to be clicking yet, keep going. You do not need a perfect track record to build something meaningful. You need honesty, reps, and enough stubbornness to keep moving when your confidence is not at full strength.

Failure is not a verdict. Sometimes it is just the part where you become harder to break.
